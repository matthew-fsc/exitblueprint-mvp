Exit Blueprint · Platform Architecture

The operating system for business transitions

How the whole system fits together — and why it gets more valuable with every engagement.

Exit Blueprint is an exit-readiness platform for M&A advisors and the lower-middle-market business owners they guide. It measures a business's Diligence Readiness Score, diagnoses the gaps that would trouble a buyer, and prescribes the remediation — over a long, repeated engagement rather than a single transaction.

This document is the map of how it is built and why it is built this way. Each section opens with the plain-English picture, then drops into the technical detail for engineers in an IN THE CODE panel. It is written to be read end-to-end by a software engineer, a CTO, an investor, a CFP or M&A advisor, and an enterprise buyer — each finding the altitude they need.

React + Vite frontend Supabase Postgres + RLS multi-tenant one compute router, two hosts deterministic scoring · AI narrative-only

The core architectural principle

The engagement is the primary object in the system.

Not the assessment. Not the document. Not the company, the AI, or the CRM. Those are all things that happen inside an engagement. The engagement — the multi-year relationship between an advisor and an owner as a business is made ready to sell — is the object every table hangs off, every workflow advances, and every feature exists to improve. Read this document with that in mind and the rest of the architecture explains itself.

01

One guarded gateway

Displays read the database directly; every change or calculation goes through a single authorized server router.

02

Firm isolation in the database

Every record carries a firm_id, and Postgres itself enforces that a firm sees only its own data.

03

Scores are deterministic

Readiness scores come from fixed, auditable rules — defensible to a buyer. AI writes the narrative, never the number.

04

Assessments are snapshots

A finished assessment is locked; re-assessing creates a new one, so progress over time is preserved.

00 · Foundations

The engagement is the product

Most software in this market is organized around a document or a one-time assessment: you fill out a questionnaire, you get a report, the relationship with the tool ends. Exit Blueprint is organized around the thing that actually lasts — the engagement, the 12-to-36-month relationship in which an advisor prepares an owner and their business for a sale.

An engagement is not an event; it is a living record. It accumulates assessments, gaps, remediation tasks, evidence, advisor decisions, meeting notes, buyer questions, and — eventually — the outcome of the deal. The assessment starts the engagement. Everything after it compounds value inside it. This is why the data model, the permissions, the workflow, and the AI are all built to serve one object rather than a scattering of features.

The practical test we apply to every proposed feature is simple: does it make the engagement better, stickier, or smarter? If it does, it belongs. If it only adds a standalone tool, it does not. That single question is what keeps the system coherent as it grows from an assessment product into the operating system for business transitions.

In the code

engagements is the hub of the schema (docs/02-data-model.md): a company is assessed repeatedly, and each assessment, task, document, and gap references an engagement, not a loose company row. Score history and deltas are first-class because the engagement — not the deal — is the unit of measurement. Every future table added to the system attaches here.

Responsible for authentication, authorization, tenancy, permissions, and auditability — the layer that answers who is asking, which firm they belong to, and what they are allowed to touch. It is the subject of sections 02–03 and section 05.

Knowledge Engine

Responsible for structured business knowledge: assessments, initiatives, advisor rationale, buyer questions, and historical outcomes. Every engagement writes into it, and it is the substrate that becomes proprietary intellectual property over time — a body of structured, outcome-linked advisory knowledge no competitor can retrofit. Section 08 covers it in depth.

Workflow Engine

The workflow is the product. Where competitors sell a scoring instrument, Exit Blueprint sells the disciplined path a business travels from first assessment to closed deal and beyond. Section 06.

Rules Engine

Deterministic business rules power scoring, recommendations, workflow progression, initiative creation, document assembly, and compliance. AI never computes facts. Sections 04 and 10.

Reasoning Engine

AI only explains. It produces narratives, executive summaries, recommendations, meeting summaries, and buyer-concern explanations — always from structured data, always labeled draft. Every recommendation carries an explainable evidence chain back to the facts that produced it. Section 09.

Collaboration Engine

The CFP owns the engagement; specialists participate. The engagement becomes the shared workspace across CFP, owner, CPA, attorney, M&A advisor, and valuation expert. Section 11.

In the code

The engines are not separate services — they are a way of reading a single codebase. Identity lives in server/functions.ts (authorize/gate/dispatch), server/auth-jwt.ts, and Postgres RLS. The Rules Engine is shared/scoring/engine.ts plus the rubric rows and server/roadmap.ts. Reasoning is server/narrative.ts behind the Anthropic boundary. Knowledge is the domain schema itself. Keeping them conceptually distinct is what stops a change in one from leaking into another.

03 · Identity Engine

A compute request, end to end

Before any action runs, the system confirms who is asking and which firm they belong to, and can refuse the action if that firm isn't entitled to it. Critically, it never trusts the browser to declare whose data to touch — the firm is looked up from the user's own record on the server.

Browser → HostPOST /functions/v1/{name} · Bearer JWT, body
↓
Host verifies JWTHS256 (Supabase) or JWKS (Clerk) → Claims{sub, role}
↓
handleFunctionCall(name, body, ctx)three gates, in order
↓
1 · authorize()resolve firm_id from profile (asUser) — never the body
2 · entitlementGate()402 if BILLING_ENFORCED & not entitled
3 · dispatch()handler(service, firmId, body)
↓
Postgres (RLS)rows scoped to the firm → JSON result (or 402/403/404)

In the code

The host verifies the bearer JWT — HS256 for Supabase Auth today, JWKS for Clerk (planned) — yielding Claims{sub, role}. handleFunctionCall(name, body, ctx) then runs three gates in order:

  1. authorize() resolves firm_id from the caller's profiles row via asUser() — never from the request body; role- and scope-sets (LEDGER_FNS, FIRM_FNS, DOC_FNS) decide what the caller may reach.
  2. entitlementGate() returns 402 for paid actions when BILLING_ENFORCED is on and the firm isn't entitled (a comp path keeps beta firms open).
  3. dispatch() runs the handler with every query executed as the caller, so Postgres RLS — not application code — is the real enforcement boundary.

04 · Rules Engine

The load-bearing rule — scoring is deterministic

The readiness scores are produced by fixed, auditable rules: the same inputs always yield the same score, so a result can be defended to a buyer or a lender. The AI writes the narrative around those numbers — the plain-English report — but it can never compute, adjust, or overwrite a score.

Assessment answersinputs · immutable snapshot
→
shared/scoring/engine.tsdeterministic · versioned
→
sub_scores → dimensionsDRS (business) · ORI (owner)
→
gaps + playbooksrule-thresholds
┊  read-only, structured  ┊
server/narrative.ts + Anthropicreads structured result only
Draft narrativelabeled draft · prompt_version'd
seed/fixtures/reference_scorer.pyCI must match the engine exactly

In the code

shared/scoring/engine.ts is a pure function of (rubric, answers), ported from seed/fixtures/reference_scorer.py, the executable reference. CI fails unless the engine reproduces the reference fixtures exactly — down to Python's round-half-even, replicated in pyRound() via BigInt so the doubles match bit-for-bit.

Flow: answers → banded sub_scores → weighted dimensions → two separate indices, DRS (business-readiness dimensions) and ORI (owner-readiness). Rule thresholds emit gaps, which instantiate playbook tasks (server/roadmap.ts).

AI boundary: server/narrative.ts reads the structured result and calls Anthropic; output is labelled draft and stamped with a prompt_version. The arrow is one-way — data flows to the model, never back to a scoring table.

06 · Workflow Engine

The workflow is the product

Competitors sell an instrument — a score, a report, a valuation. Exit Blueprint sells the path. The most valuable thing the platform does is carry a business, an owner, and their advisory team through the same disciplined sequence every time, so nothing is skipped and every stage builds on the last. That sequence is the product; the score is just one station on it.

Discovery — understand the business, owner, and financial picture
Assessment — deterministic DRS + ORI, named gaps
Planning — prioritized initiatives, sprints, hand-offs
Execution — remediation tasks, evidence collection
Continuous Evaluation — readiness updates as things change
Specialist Collaboration — CPA, attorney, valuation expert
Deal Preparation — CIM, teaser, buyer package assembled
Due Diligence — buyer questions, verified binder
Transaction — the close
Post-Close — outcome captured, fed back into the system

Because the workflow is data, not a static diagram, the system knows which stage an engagement is in, what is blocking progression, and what should happen next. That is the difference between a tool an advisor uses and a system that runs the practice. It is also the hardest thing for a competitor to copy, because it encodes how good advisors actually work.

In the code

Workflow state lives on the engagement and its children, not in UI. The five sell-side work streams (docs/17) map onto CEPA's Discover → Prepare → Decide gates and the CFP 7-step loop (docs/18); progression is driven by deterministic rules — a gap cleared, evidence verified, an assessment superseded — not by an advisor manually flipping a status. server/roadmap.ts turns gaps into playbook task sets stamped with a playbook_version.

07 · Workflow Engine

Continuous evaluation — the system asks "what changed?"

Competitors perform periodic assessments: you sit down once a quarter or once a year, re-run the questionnaire, and get a fresh score. Exit Blueprint performs continuous evaluation. Instead of asking, once in a while, "how ready is the business?", the system is always asking a sharper question: "what changed, and what did it do to readiness?"

Financial changesrevenue, margin, concentration
Management changeskey-person, org depth
Customer concentrationa top account shifts
Completed initiativesa gap is remediated
Buyer diligencenew questions surface risk
Ownership changescap table, intent
↓  every meaningful change  ↓
Readiness updates automaticallynew immutable assessment · delta vs. prior · re-prioritized roadmap

This matters commercially as much as technically. An engagement that re-scores only at renewal is easy to cancel and easy to forget. An engagement that continuously reflects the real state of the business becomes the advisor's — and the owner's — source of truth, and the cost of leaving it climbs every month. Continuous evaluation is what turns a one-time assessment into a living relationship.

In the code

Because assessments are immutable and versioned, "re-evaluate" is always "create a new snapshot and diff it against the last" — never a mutation. Score history and deltas are first-class (rule 4). External n8n workflows call authenticated webhook endpoints (stale-engagements, stalled-tasks, reassessment-due) that this repo exposes; a completed task, a verified document, or a changed ledger figure can each open a fresh evaluation rather than waiting for a scheduled review.

08 · Knowledge Engine

Structured knowledge becomes proprietary IP

Anyone can rebuild the scoring engine, the reports, and the portal. What cannot be copied is the structured record of what actually happened across thousands of engagements — the gaps that mattered, the initiatives that moved the price, the questions buyers really asked, and how deals actually closed. The Knowledge Engine's job is to capture all of that as data, from day one, so it compounds into an asset no new entrant can retrofit.

The design discipline is that everything an advisor knows or decides is captured as structured fact, not free text buried in a note. An assessment is structured. A gap is structured. A remediation initiative is structured. A buyer's diligence question is structured. And — the highest-value record of all — a deal's outcome is structured: the final enterprise value, multiple, EBITDA at close, buyer type, and which risks the buyer actually flagged.

Outcome calibrationpredicted DRS vs. actual EV — the FICO moat
Verified financial corpusledger-verified, not self-reported
The engagement graphgap → remediation → score → price

Turned on early and left to accumulate, this record lets the DRS graduate from an opinion to a calibrated prediction — "businesses at DRS 72 closed at 4.6–5.4× within 14 months, 82% of the time" — and lets playbook advice be grounded in the platform's own results rather than generic best practice. That is the Knowledge Engine turning into intellectual property.

In the code

The moat substrate is deal_outcomes (docs/09-moats.md): one record per closed or broken engagement, tying the prediction (DRS, ORI, verified %, predicted EV range) to reality (final EV, multiple, EBITDA, buyer type, structure, retrade, days-to-close, buyer-flagged risks). Two non-negotiables protect it: capture is advisor-reported fact, never inferred by a model; and calibration informs the rubric — it never edits a score directly. A recalibration ships as a new rubric_version / valuation_rules_version. Cross-firm aggregation is anonymized and opt-in; RLS stays firm-scoped on the raw rows.

09 · Reasoning Engine

The AI architecture — three layers

The architecture is intentionally split into three layers, and the split is the whole point: it lets Exit Blueprint use AI aggressively for what AI is good at (explaining) while keeping it entirely away from what it is bad at (being the system of record for a number a buyer will scrutinize).

Layer 1 · Deterministic system

Facts · scoring · business logicrule-based, versioned, auditable — the source of truth
↓  structured data flows up, one-way

Layer 2 · Reasoning

Narratives · recommendations · summariesAI explains the facts — always labeled draft
↓  every engagement contributes

Layer 3 · Learning

Advisor rationale · completed transactions · buyer questions · successful initiatives · industry intelligenceknowledge compounds over time and improves layers 1 & 2

Layer 1 is the deterministic system — facts, scoring, business logic. Layer 2 is reasoning — the narratives, executive summaries, recommendations, and meeting summaries the AI produces from those facts. Layer 3 is learning — the advisor rationale, completed transactions, buyer questions, and successful initiatives that accumulate as structured knowledge and feed back into sharper rules and better-grounded reasoning. Crucially, learning never rewrites a score in place; it informs the next versioned rubric.

The rule that keeps all three honest: every recommendation should have an explainable evidence chain back to the deterministic facts that produced it. AI in this system is never a black box that emits a verdict — it is a writer that shows its work.

In the code

server/narrative.ts is the only path to the Anthropic API, and the key never reaches the browser. Every call is logged to an llm_calls cost ledger against a versioned prompt registry (prompt_version). A narrative guard rejects any drafted number that is not present in the source structured data — the model cannot smuggle a computed figure into a report. Output is always stamped draft until an advisor accepts it.

10 · Rules + Reasoning

Documents are assembled, never authored

Every important document in an exit — the CIM, the executive summary, the teaser, the buyer package, the quarterly review, the readiness report, the due-diligence responses — is assembled from structured data, not written from a blank page. The facts come from the deterministic system; the narrative connective tissue comes from AI; the advisor approves. No document is ever hand-authored in a way that could drift from the underlying record.

Deterministic factsscores, gaps, verified financials, evidence
+
AI narrativeprose from the facts, labeled draft
→
Assembled documentCIM · teaser · buyer package · DD responses · quarterly review

This is why the same engagement can produce a one-page teaser and a hundred-page diligence binder that never contradict each other: they are two renderings of one source of truth. It is also why a number in a buyer package can always be traced back to the assessment and the evidence that produced it — assembly preserves provenance in a way authoring never could.

In the code

Document handlers live under server/documents/ and server/pdf.ts. A document reads the deterministic result and the verified data room, calls narrative.ts for prose, and renders — carrying the prompt_version, rubric_version, and evidence references with it. Pluggable seams keep vendors out of the core: a ParserAdapter handles document extraction (manual default; Reducto/LlamaParse selectable) and a StorageAdapter handles bytes (DB blob now, object storage later).

11 · Collaboration Engine

The engagement as shared workspace

An exit is a team sport. The CFP owns the engagement and the client relationship, but the work pulls in a CPA, an attorney, an M&A advisor, a valuation expert — and the owner themselves. Exit Blueprint makes the engagement the shared workspace those roles collaborate inside, rather than a document that gets emailed around and forks into five inconsistent copies.

↓ invites & scopes
Ownerthe business

Because everyone works inside the same structured engagement, a valuation expert's input, an attorney's flagged contract, and an owner's uploaded evidence all land in the same record and feed the same continuous evaluation. The advisor stays the trusted relationship manager; the platform is the shared surface that keeps the whole team honest and in sync.

In the code

Roles are enforced the same way tenancy is: app.user_role() in RLS plus route guards (RequireAdvisor / RequireStaff / RequireOwner in App.tsx) and scope-sets in server/functions.ts. A participant is a profiles row tied to the firm and scoped to an engagement — the collaboration model is an extension of the identity model, not a separate system, which is why it inherits the same firm isolation and auditability.

12 · Strategy in the architecture

Build the operating system, integrate the commodity

The single most important strategic decision in the architecture is what to build and what to integrate. Exit Blueprint builds the things that are proprietary, compounding, and specific to business transitions. It integrates the mature commodity software that everyone already uses and no one would switch for. The goal is to be the operating system for the engagement — not to re-implement a CRM or an accounting package that does its job perfectly well.

Exit Blueprint owns

Proprietary · compounding · unique to transitions

  • Knowledge graph
  • Rules engine
  • Advisor library
  • Buyer library
  • Reasoning

Why draw the line here? Rebuilding a CRM or a general ledger burns engineering on problems the market already solved and creates nothing defensible — you would ship a worse QuickBooks. Owning the workflow, the knowledge graph, and the reasoning creates assets that get better with every engagement and that no integration can replicate. Every hour spent building commodity software is an hour not spent widening the moat.

In the code

The architecture is built for integration without vendor lock-in: external services are reached server-side only and behind adapters. Ledger access is OAuth to QuickBooks/Xero; billing is Stripe; identity is moving to Clerk via JWKS (docs/24) without touching the RLS model, because policies key on a text subject claim, not a Supabase-specific one. Nothing in the core scoring, workflow, or knowledge code depends on which commodity vendor is plugged in.

13 · Enterprise readiness

Security is foundational, not a feature

Exit Blueprint holds the most sensitive data a business owner has — financials, contracts, cap tables, and the fact that they intend to sell. That data will be scrutinized by institutional buyers and their diligence teams. Security here is not a checklist bolted on before a sale; it is a property of the architecture, present from the first migration.

SOC 2 Type IIthe target attestation
RLSfirm isolation in the database
RBACrole-scoped access
Audit logswho did what, when
MFA · SSOenterprise identity
Encryptionin transit & at rest
Version historynothing silently overwritten
Immutable assessmentstamper-evident record
Least privilegeeach role sees only its scope

Why these matter. RLS means a bug in application code cannot leak one firm's data to another, because isolation is enforced by the database, not by the developer remembering a WHERE clause. Immutable assessments and version history mean the record is tamper-evident — exactly what a buyer's counsel needs to trust a diligence binder. RBAC and least privilege keep a specialist scoped to their lane. SOC 2 Type II, MFA, SSO, and encryption are the entry ticket to selling into firms that will not touch a vendor without them. Building these in from day one is far cheaper than retrofitting them, and it is what makes the platform sellable to an enterprise at all.

In the code

RLS is enforced in Postgres via app.user_role() / app.user_firm_id() on every table; the compute router runs every query as the caller so the database is the enforcement boundary. Immutability ships as a migration (20260718000200_immutable_assessments.sql). Identity moves to Clerk (MFA/SSO) via JWKS without a schema rewrite. Audit and review-queue handlers record privileged actions. Security posture and vendor-diligence answers are documented in docs/13, docs/15, and docs/16.

14 · Technical roadmap

Three phases, one direction

The build order follows directly from the strategy: earn the right to own more of the workflow by first owning the engagement, then expand only where the platform creates leverage the incumbents cannot. Each phase is a wider circle drawn around the same center.

Phase 1

Own the engagement

Become the system of record for the advisory relationship — assessment, deterministic scoring, gaps, roadmap, evidence, deliverables, continuous evaluation. Turn on outcome capture from day one so the knowledge asset starts compounding immediately.

Phase 2

Own high-value workflow modules

Extend into the specialized, transition-specific work that generic tools do badly — document assembly, the buyer/diligence library, specialist collaboration, calibration analytics. These deepen the moat because they feed on the Phase 1 knowledge graph.

Phase 3

Replace commodity capabilities only where platform leverage exists

Absorb a commodity capability only when owning it inside the engagement creates leverage an integration cannot — never for its own sake. The default remains integrate; replacement is the exception that must justify itself.

Why this order? Owning the engagement first is what generates the proprietary data every later phase depends on. Chasing commodity replacement early would spend scarce engineering on undifferentiated work and starve the moat. Sequencing outward from the engagement means every phase is built on an asset the previous phase created.

15 · The moat

The knowledge flywheel

Everything above exists to turn one loop. Every engagement generates structured data; that data sharpens the rules and grounds the reasoning; sharper rules and better reasoning make the next engagement more valuable; a more valuable engagement attracts more advisors and produces more data. The loop is the company. Assessment scores, reports, and portals are features a competitor can copy in a quarter — the compounding institutional knowledge this loop produces is the thing they cannot.

Assessment→ Advisor decisions→ Initiatives→ Buyer questions→ Due diligence→ Transaction outcomes→ Advisor feedback→ Knowledge graph→ Improved rules→ Improved AI→ Better future engagements↺

The critical property is that this knowledge cannot be retrofitted. Calibrating a readiness score against real outcomes requires years of closed deals tied to prior predictions; a well-funded competitor starting today cannot buy those years. That is why the architecture insists on capturing structured outcomes from the very first engagement, even before there is enough data to be useful — because the moat is the accumulation, and the accumulation only starts once.

In the code

The flywheel is made real by three disciplines already in the schema: deal_outcomes captures the transaction result (docs/09); immutable, versioned assessments preserve the prediction to calibrate against; and every recalibration ships as a new rubric_version so the score stays deterministic even as it gets smarter. The knowledge compounds in data; the rules and prompts are re-versioned from it — the loop never violates rule 1 or rule 2.

17 · Closing

Engineering philosophy

Every engineering decision in Exit Blueprint is measured against the same set of principles. They are not aspirations on a wall — they are the reasons the architecture looks the way it does, and the test any future change has to pass.

01The engagement is the product.
02Facts are deterministic.
03AI explains; it never computes.
04Workflow is the competitive advantage.
05Knowledge compounds.
06Security is foundational.
07Integrate commodity software.
08Build proprietary workflow.
09Every feature should increase stickiness.
10Every engagement should improve the next one.

Hold these together and the direction is unambiguous: Exit Blueprint is not building an assessment tool that happens to store some data. It is building the operating system for business transitions — a system where the facts are defensible, the AI is trustworthy because it only ever explains, the workflow is the moat, and every engagement quietly makes the next one better. Build to reinforce that, and the company compounds.
